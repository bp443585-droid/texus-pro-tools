1. Theoretical Chronology: From Social Statistics to the World Health Organization Standard

The mathematical organization of relative body weights traces back to early sociological research models designed in 1832 by the prominent Belgian mathematician Adolphe Quetelet. Seeking to establish the physical metrics of standard human developments across broader cultural cohorts, Quetelet discovered that past infancy, adult weight tracking scales uniformly in perfect relation to height dimensions squared. This relationship became known across health houses as the historic Quetelet Index of body composition.

Following systematic verification loops driven by metabolic clinics in 1972, research teams confirmed this index as an effective indirect diagnostic indicator for absolute body fat percentages. This confirmation led to the international standardization of Body Mass Index scales by the World Health Organization (WHO), building the core reference slabs used across healthcare databases today.
